Chicago Blue Line Train Tracker

An LED display of Chicago's Blue Line that indicates which stations a train is currently at. I used an ESP32-WROVER dev board to send a request every few seconds to CTA's API for each train's current position. The microcontroller then parses that information and sends the appropriate bits to the pixels. Each pair of pixels is a station on the Blue Line. The innermost line of pixels are for the trains heading South, and the outermost are for the trains heading North.

Handwired Mechanical Keyboard

After taking an interest in mechanical keyboards I wondered if I could wire and program my own. I took red Kailh box switches, sodered them into a matrix, and connected them to a Rasberry Pi Pico. The Pico scans the matrix to register and print out keystrokes.

Automatic Trash Collector

This was a project for my Intro to Engineering Design class that I worked on with 6 other students. It is an automatic trash collecting robot that uses the Ultralytics YOLOv8 object detection model to detect trash. When it detects trash, it calculates how far it is, moves to it, and uses its grabber to pick it up and dump it in the bin.

Self-driving Car

This was a project for my Embedded Control class where I programmed the MSP430 on this RSLK robotic car to navigate through a maze with PID control. It takes readings from an ultrasonic sensor, a compass, and bumpers to avoid obstacles and adjust its position as it drives.

Heart Rate Detector

This circuit detects a hearbeat by measuring the differences in light that pass through a finger. The small signal from the infrared transistor is fed to amplifiers and filters. The output is connected to LEDs that light up depending on the frequency of the heart rate. The yellow LED lights up whenever it detects a heartbeat, and the red LED only blinks when the heart rate is within the normal resting human heart rate (between 60-100 beats per minute).

Line Follower

This line follower is made up of two 555 timers that each control a motor. The duty cycle of the PWM signals generated by the astable 555 timers changes depending on how much light from the LED reaches the photoresistor. The difference in how much light is reflected between a black and white surface changes the average voltage of the PWM signals that is proportional to the speed of the wheels.
